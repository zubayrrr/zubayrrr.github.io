OKRs stands for Objectives &amp; Key Results   
   
OKRs are a framework to help you phrase &amp; achieve goals:   
   
• Objectives tell you where to go   
   
• Key Results are quantified milestones to get there   
   
• Projects are what you'll do to achieve your Key Results

Objectives set the direction:   
   
• Stretch yourself &amp; think big   
   
• Make them meaningful - no jargon    
   
• Easy to communicate - memorable &amp; inspiring   
   
Example: become a top 5 online ski sock retailer

Key Results are the milestones to get you there:   
   
• Quantify your objective   
   
• Help you track progress   
   
• Should measure outcome, not output   
   
• Always contain a metric, baseline &amp; target   
   
Example: Q4 revenue of $1.4M in Colorado

Projects are what you'll do to reach your Key Results:   
   
• Break projects down into tasks   
   
• Manage the smallest possible unit   
   
• Measure progress and follow up weekly   
   
Example:    
   
• Project: Christmas sale email sequence targeting CO   
   
• Task: create the email header visual

Work in 90-day sprints:   
   
• Track project progress weekly   
   
• Plan projects against Key Results quarterly   
   
• Set objectives based on strategy &amp; goals annually   
   
Focus on the next quarter helps you navigate uncertainty &amp; prioritize.

5 best practices from 10 years of setting OKRs:   
   
• Use a dashboard for communication &amp; transparency   
   
• OKRs can't fix a bad strategy - address that first   
   
• The bigger the goal, the smaller the steps   
   
• 3-5 annual objectives &amp; quarterly OKRs   
   
• One owner per OKR, followed weekly

Establish product-market fit.

FAQ part 2:   
   
Isn't this just a fancy word for goals?   
   
Yes, but:   
   
• OKRs help you set priority   
   
• They define the outcome   
   
• The path to getting there   
   
OKRs is a framework to help you phrase &amp; achieve goals.

FAQ part 3:   
   
What's the difference between KPIs and OKRs?   
   
• KPIs measure the performance of an existing process   
   
• OKRs help capture a new opportunity or solve a problem   
   
The highest-performing organizations use both!
